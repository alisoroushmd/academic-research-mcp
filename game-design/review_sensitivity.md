# Sensitivity & Ethics Review — Crescent Imperium v1

Reviewer: sensitivity reader / ethics consultant. Scope: v1 design doc only; no playtest observed.

The design is unusually thoughtful for a 4X of this subject. Legitimacy-as-currency, attributed Suffering, and doctrine-not-tech are all good instincts. The concerns below are about whether those instincts are load-bearing enough for what this game actually puts on the table.

## 1. The Regional Suffering track
Making civilian harm a shared, attributed penalty is better than ignoring it — but a single track still invites optimization ("how much Suffering can I afford before the penalty bites?"). Labyrinth keeps the cost *local* (Prestige, Funding, specific country governance) so players feel the political consequence of each specific event; This War of Mine refuses to let the player optimize at all — narrative beats interrupt the economy. Crescent Imperium currently sits closer to Labyrinth, but by bundling all violence into one meter it risks a TI4-style "acceptable losses" read.

Safeguards to consider: (a) non-fungible suffering — refugee, displacement, and mass-casualty tokens that have distinct downstream effects rather than stacking on one bar; (b) narrative cards drawn at thresholds that name a specific historical analogue and force a table pause; (c) remove the "pyrrhic victory" proportional split — if the track maxes, *no one wins*. Make it a loss condition, not a scoring adjustment. A shared penalty you can still win through is an optimization target.

## 2. The Movement faction as AQ/ISIS
A player will sit down wanting to win as ISIS. The design has to decide whether that's acceptable and, if so, what it asks of it. The identity-swap (PLO → Hezbollah → AQ → ISIS) is half-feature, half-dodge: it's honest that these movements are historically distinct, but it lets the designer avoid confronting that the AQ/ISIS eras are *categorically different* from the PLO era in targeting of civilians. Treating them as points on one faction's continuum flattens a real moral distinction.

Recommendations: the Movement's victory track should actively penalize mass-casualty terror against civilians (not just cost Legitimacy — *remove* victory progress), so that "winning as ISIS" requires playing against ISIS's actual methods. Consider making the ISIS-era card an *opt-in module* with a content warning and an alternative (e.g., a generic "Insurgency" card) for tables that don't want it.

## 3. Playing The Islamic Republic opposite an Iranian-American friend
This is the hardest real-table problem and the design is silent on it. A game where you score points by "ideological export" while someone at the table has family affected by 1988, the Green Movement, or Mahsa Amini is not the same game for both players. Same issue with a Syrian playing across from The Ba'ath, a Kurd across from The Republic, a Palestinian across from The State. The design needs an explicit session-zero framework (see §6) and should consider faction-assignment guidance — e.g., "draft with veto" rather than random assignment.

## 4. Symmetric Proxy slots — Mossad = IRGC-QF = ISIS cells?
This is the clearest false equivalence in the draft. A state intelligence station operating under legal cover is not the same game object as a non-state armed cell, and treating them symmetrically launders the distinction. Defensible as abstraction *only* if the exposure/consequence side is asymmetric: exposing a state proxy costs Legitimacy and invites diplomatic response; exposing a non-state proxy can collapse a sanctuary or trigger a decapitation event. Right now the slot reads as mechanically identical. Fix: keep one slot, but make proxy *types* carry different vulnerabilities, different legitimacy costs when used against civilians, and different attribution rules.

## 5. Israel/Palestine — The missing faction
Naming Israel as "The State" while Palestinians exist only as a historical phase of The Movement is the single largest representational problem in v1. It encodes Palestinians as sub-state and transient by design. This will read — correctly — as erasure.

Minimum fix: Palestinians as their own faction or dual-faction (PA / Hamas or a unified "The People" with internal factional tension), with their own victory track (recognition, return, sovereignty), their own Legitimacy dynamics, and the ability to exist across all five Eras. If the designer cannot figure out how to model this, that's a signal the game is not ready, not a reason to omit them.

## 6. Faction naming — distancing or erasure?
Root's "Marquise / Eyrie / Woodland Alliance" works because Root is a fable. "The Kingdom / The State / The Republic" for real, named, currently-governing entities reads as plausible-deniability cosplay rather than abstraction — everyone at the table knows exactly who they are. Pick one: either fully fictionalize (different map, invented names) or name them. The current middle ground gets the costs of both.

## 7. Group-play failure modes & safety tools
The game ships into living rooms with mixed-background tables and no facilitator. It must ship with: a session-zero script; X-card and lines-and-veils as standard; content warnings keyed to specific Era cards (1982 Lebanon, 1988, 2003, 2014, 2023–); an opt-out rule for any specific Era or faction; and guidance on faction drafting with veto. These are not optional appendices — they belong in the rulebook's first chapter. See Alabaster / Monsterhearts / Honey Heist's safety tooling as precedent for hobby-game-legible framing.

## 8. Things the game should refuse to mechanize
- Specific, nameable massacres or atrocities as card text with point values.
- Sexual violence, in any abstraction.
- Specific religious/ethnic populations as resources extracted or converted (population is not a commodity — avoid "convert Shi'a population" style card text).
- Nuclear *use* on populated hexes as a scoring action. Nuclear latency as deterrent, yes; nuclear strike as a playable move with a point value, no.
- Named living political figures as assassination targets with mechanical payoff.
- The Holocaust, the Nakba, and the Armenian Genocide as mechanical events. They can exist as board state/context; they cannot be triggered by player action for advantage.

---

## Must-fix before external playtest
1. Add Palestinian faction (or equivalent structural fix to §5).
2. Suffering track maxing = loss condition, not a proportional penalty.
3. Asymmetric proxy exposure/consequence rules (§4).
4. Mass-casualty terror removes Movement victory progress, does not merely cost Legitimacy.
5. Ship session-zero, X-card, lines-and-veils, and per-card content warnings in the core rulebook.
6. Explicit refusal list in the designer's notes (§8) published alongside the rules.
7. Resolve the naming question — commit to named factions or fully fictionalize.

## Should-discuss in design group
- Whether AQ/ISIS era is opt-in module vs. base game.
- Faction-draft with veto vs. random assignment; designer's guidance to hosts on mixed-background tables.
- Whether the game wants a non-player "civil society" track (journalists, NGOs, diaspora) that can push back on all factions.
- Whether 1976 is the right start date or if pre-'76 context (Sykes-Picot, '48, '67, '73) should be acknowledged in setup rather than baked as invisible initial conditions.
- Consultation plan: this design needs paid regional consultants (Iranian, Palestinian, Israeli, Iraqi, Syrian, Kurdish, Turkish, Saudi, Egyptian voices at minimum) before it sees a wider table, not after.

The game is worth making. It is not yet ready to be played.
