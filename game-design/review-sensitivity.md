# Review: CRESCENT v1 — Sensitivity and Design Honesty

## 1. Abstraction honesty

The archetypes are transparent, and pretending otherwise is the first problem. My readings:

- **Petro-Sovereign**: Saudi Arabia, with spillover onto the UAE and Kuwait. The "megaproject cards → prestige VP" is NEOM/Vision 2030 in everything but name.
- **Revolutionary Republic**: Iran. "Export the Cause," sanction-ratchet vulnerability, and the ideological alignment scoring pin this precisely.
- **Merchant Emirate**: Dubai/Qatar composite. "Safe Harbour" hosting other players' assets is explicitly the Gulf free-zone / sovereign-wealth role.
- **Contested Republic**: Lebanon primarily (the A/B/C/D quadripartite explicitly echoes the Ta'if settlement), with Iraq and Syria smeared on top. The "reconciliation track" is confessionalism-as-endgame.
- **Settler Republic**: Israel. "Settler" in the name, tech edge, "Deterrent," nuclear-analogue on the tech ladder, legitimacy-capped trade partners — this is not archetypal. It is a portrait.
- **Outside Power**: US, with Russia and (increasingly) China as reskins.

So: one mapping is archetypal (Petro-Sovereign generalises across Gulf monarchies), two are composite-but-recognisable (Merchant Emirate, Contested Republic), and two are effectively 1:1 (Revolutionary Republic, Settler Republic). The abstraction is real for some factions and cosmetic for others. That asymmetry is the honest problem. When the shoe fits one player tightly and another loosely, the design has implicitly decided which nation's agency is "a type of actor" and which is "the specific actor we all know." The Settler Republic especially — calling it "Settler" is a political claim, not a neutral archetype. Either commit to that claim or rename it ("Fortress Republic," "Garrison State") and actually generalise the mechanics so they'd fit, say, apartheid-era South Africa or a hypothetical nuclear Taiwan. Right now the label does the heavy political work while the mechanics pretend to be abstract.

## 2. Missing perspectives

Present only as resources or modifiers: civilians (Population tiles), refugees (an Era Card effect), religious communities (Holy Site tiles, the Appeal action), labour (unmentioned — Gulf migrant labour is the economic substrate of the Petro-Sovereign and Merchant Emirate and is invisible here), women (entirely absent), diaspora (a card subtype for the Merchant Emirate).

The design is a room of states looking at people. For 90 minutes that is a defensible scope choice — *Twilight Struggle* is also a room of two states looking at the world — but CRESCENT is louder about "exposing trade-offs real regional actors navigate," and the actors it names are all sovereigns. I would strongly consider one of: (a) a playable Diaspora/Transnational faction whose VP comes from remittances, advocacy, and moving people across the board; (b) a non-state Civil Society faction that scores on Stability and Legitimacy rather than controlling them; (c) at minimum, a neutral "Population" pseudo-player whose welfare track produces negative VP for whoever destabilises them, so civilians are not just a resource yield. Without at least one of these, the game's implicit claim is that the Middle East's story is the story of its ruling classes. That is the claim the region's own writers have spent forty years arguing against.

## 3. Specific flags

- **Refugee Wave Era Card**: the most dangerous single element. If it functions as a cost modifier or a Stability debuff, it treats displacement as weather. Refugees should at minimum move tokens (Population) that remain on the board, change regional Legitimacy, and remember who caused them. If the card is reversible by the next Era, it is trivialising.
- **Holy Site resource tile**: calling a sacred place a "resource tile" is already the problem. If Holy Sites are mechanically identical to Oil or Trade with a different icon, cut them. If they gate Legitimacy and Appeal specifically, and cannot be "harvested," keep them — but rename to something like Sacred Ground and make their destruction/contestation carry a Legitimacy cost to all players, not just the attacker.
- **A/B/C/D blocs in the Contested Republic**: the abstraction here is probably correct — naming Sunni/Shia/Kurdish in the spec and then hiding it behind letters in play is worse than either extreme. Pick one. If the letters are genuinely interchangeable, the civil-war mechanic has no teeth. I would make them asymmetric but fictional (four invented confessional/ethnic names) so the mechanics bite without the real names.
- **Tech ladder culminating in a nuclear-analogue**: only one faction can climb it in a way that matters. This is the Settler Republic's private lever dressed as a shared track. Either make nuclear acquisition reachable (and terrible) for multiple factions — which is the actual regional dynamic — or remove the analogue. As written, it is a single-player power fantasy with deniability.
- **Appeal action using religious media**: the mechanic itself is fine and arguably necessary; soft power through religious broadcasting is how legitimacy actually moves in the region. The risk is the flavour text. Keep it structural (Legitimacy cost to caster, Legitimacy shift in target region) and do not let card art or names valorise any specific clerical figure, living or dead.

## 4. What the design gets right

The no-military-victory rule, shared Stability bonus, and attribution/Information trade-off are genuinely load-bearing and smarter than most games in this space. "Military degrades the Stability that pays you" is the single best thing here — it is the lesson Gulf planners actually internalised after 2003/2015 and it is rare to see it modelled. The attribution rule (public by default, Information to obscure) is structurally honest about how covert action works and costs.

But load-bearing is not the same as sufficient. The Stability bonus is a multiplier on average regional Stability — which means a player who controls the highest-yield regions can absorb stability loss elsewhere. I would make the bonus scale with the *minimum* regional Stability, not the average, so the worst-off region sets the ceiling for everyone. That single change converts "shared fate" from rhetoric into a constraint.

## 5. Should this be made?

Yes, with two changes.

First, rename the Settler Republic and genuinely generalise its kit, or keep the name and drop the pretence of archetype throughout (commit to being a game about this region, by name, with sources in the rulebook). Half-abstraction is the worst option.

Second, add one non-state playable role — Diaspora or Civil Society — so the game is not exclusively about sovereigns adjudicating populations. Ninety minutes is enough for five asymmetric factions; it is enough for six.

If those changes are unacceptable, the adjacent design that would be defensible is narrower: a two-to-three player game about oil-market interdependence and sanction regimes, set in the same stylised basin, with no Holy Sites, no Refugee Wave, no nuclear track. That game could be excellent and would carry none of this one's freight.
