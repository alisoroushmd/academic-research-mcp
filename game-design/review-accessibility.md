# Crescent v1 — Accessibility & Teachability Review

Reviewer lens: rulebook editor and con-demo runner. Audience: Eurogame-literate
(Terra Mystica, Brass: Birmingham, Great Western Trail), not conflict-sim-literate.

## 1. Rules complexity estimate

I count **twelve** live subsystems: (1) five resources with distinct economies,
(2) 12-region map with stability + legitimacy-holder + tile yields, (3) shared
8-slot rondel with scaling cost, (4) proxy tokens with stability spillover, (5)
public-vs-covert attribution via Information, (6) Era Cards as era-long modifiers,
(7) dual-layer Legitimacy (internal track + per-region), (8) Military cap + non-
recovering expenditure, (9) Stability track with shared-fate scoring, (10)
volatile global oil market, (11) hidden objectives + Information tokens, (12)
five asymmetric factions with vuln + private lever.

Brass: Birmingham has ~6 (network, resource market, link/build, era turnover,
loans/income track, cards). Crescent is roughly **2x Brass**. That puts it
outside one SD of Brass and inside COIN territory — call it **COIN-light**:
less chrome than *A Distant Plain*, more than *Pax Pamir 2e*. Closest Euro
comparable is *On Mars* or *Kanban EV* for subsystem count, but Crescent has
hidden info and faction asymmetry those games don't.

## 2. Teach time

Realistically **40–55 minutes** for four Euro-literate players, not 20. The
three stall points:

1. **Internal vs. regional Legitimacy.** Two tracks named the same thing, gating
   different actions. Needs a worked example ("if you Appeal into Region 4,
   *you* lose 1 internal Legitimacy and the *region's* holder may flip").
2. **Attribution / Information economy.** "Public actions are free, covert costs
   Info" is fine, but players will ask "why would I ever go covert?" until you
   walk them through how public Military hits their regional Legitimacy in
   regions they want to trade into. That is a 5-minute detour.
3. **Era Card timing and the Stability shared bonus.** Players conflate era-card
   modifiers with era-end scoring and miss that *everyone* scores from average
   Stability, which changes their attitude toward Proxy. Expect a re-explain
   after the first Market phase.

## 3. First-game trap

Yes, and it's cleanly designed in: **"Spam Proxy tokens for cheap power
projection."** Proxy is the most legible aggressive action on the rondel, Info
can be hoarded, and on turn 1 it reads like Brass's "build the cheap industry
first." By Era 3 you have destabilised the regions you planned to trade
through, the Stability bonus scores negative-relative for you, and the
Contested Republic player has turned half your proxies into their
reconciliation-track VP. This is the *Terra Mystica "I'll just terraform
everything"* trap, correctly placed.

## 4. Asymmetry load

**Too heavy for a true first play as written.** Root is the right warning:
five wildly different action economies fracture the teach. Crescent is better
than Root because the **rondel is shared** — that's the Terra Mystica save —
but each faction still layers (special action + vulnerability + private lever
+ starting mix). That's four deltas per faction; Terra Mystica has ~two.
Recommendation: ship a "first-game factions" subset of three (Petro-Sovereign,
Merchant Emirate, Settler Republic — the most legible economies) and gate
Revolutionary Republic and Contested Republic behind game two. Scythe did this
correctly; Root did not.

## 5. Information overload

Hidden objectives + hidden Info tokens + era cards + oil price + five
asymmetric private levers is **one hidden layer too many** for game one. The
point of the design — that you plan under structural uncertainty — survives if
you drop **hidden objectives in the first game only** and reveal private
levers. Keep Info tokens hidden; they're the core of the attribution mechanic.
Hidden objectives are the layer players can least price without a reference
game.

## 6. "What do I do on turn 1" as Contested Republic

**Not legible from the rules as written.** The faction starts with four
internal blocs that must stay balanced, is legally targetable by other players'
Proxies, and has a reconciliation private lever that only pays out at Era 5.
A new player reading section 1.4 does not know whether to (a) Diplomacy to
shore up internal Legitimacy, (b) Trade to buy insulation, or (c) play
defensive Military. The rules need a **"suggested opening" sidebar** per
faction — half a paragraph each, the way *Spirit Island* does. This is the
single highest-ROI fix in the document.

## 7. Two simplifications, two sacred cows

**Simplify:**
- **Collapse internal and regional Legitimacy into one track with a region
  tag.** The bookkeeping delta is small; the teach delta is large.
- **Reduce the rondel from 8 to 6 actions** by folding Appeal into Diplomacy
  (cost variant) and Information into Proxy (as a covert toggle). Eight slots
  is Antiquity-territory and most are redundant on turn 1.

**Do NOT simplify:**
- **Public attribution of Military.** This is the thematic spine — that
  military action is legible and costly. Removing it makes Crescent a
  generic area-control Euro.
- **The Stability shared-fate bonus.** Without it, the Proxy trap stops
  being a trap and the "destabilising a neighbour is rarely free" thesis
  collapses. This is the mechanism that earns the design's moral claim.

**Bottom line:** Teachable in ~45 min with a rewrite of the faction section,
a first-game subset, and one merged track. As-is, it's a second-play game
pretending to be a first-play game.
