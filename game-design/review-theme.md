# Review: does CRESCENT dramatise the region, or just decorate it?

The v1 spec is unusually honest about what it's trying to do, and the Eurogame-shaped skeleton is defensible. But several of the dynamics it names in the flavour text are not actually generated by the mechanics — they're asserted. Here is the dynamic-by-dynamic test.

## 1. Oil curse
**Produced?** Partially. The Petro-Sovereign's +2 Oil / -1 Legitimacy baseline and the megaproject conversion do encode the resource-for-prestige tradeoff. But there's no mechanic whereby oil income actively *crowds out* institution-building; a smart Petro player can just buy megaprojects *and* invest in Legitimacy.
**Fix:** Add a **Rentier Rule**: any Era in which your Oil income exceeds X, your Diplomacy and Invest(institution) actions cost +1 on the rondel. This forces the real tradeoff — cheap money makes slow institutional work politically unattractive.

## 2. Sanctions resilience
**Produced?** Weakly. The Revolutionary Republic has a ratcheting sanction track as a vulnerability, but there's no mechanic rewarding the sanctioned player for *reorganising around* sanctions — which is the whole historical lesson from Iran 1979–2025.
**Fix:** When the sanction track ratchets, unlock a **Shadow Economy** action: the sanctioned player gets a discounted Proxy action and can convert Oil→Influence at a black-market rate unavailable to others. Sanctions should make a faction *weirder and more dangerous*, not just poorer.

## 3. Proxy warfare as externalised conflict
**Produced?** Yes — this is the design's strongest suit. Proxy tokens in the Contested Republic, the Stability penalty, the attribution/Information interplay: all of this lets Iran-in-Lebanon, Saudi-in-Yemen, Russia-in-Syria emerge as player behaviour. The shared Stability bonus is a clever brake. Keep as-is.

## 4. Legitimacy competition
**Produced?** Structurally present (regional Legitimacy holders, the Appeal action), but flat. Every faction competes for the *same* Legitimacy currency, which collapses four different claims — revolutionary, dynastic, nationalist, religious — into one score.
**Fix:** Split Legitimacy into **three colours** (Religious, Revolutionary, Dynastic/Nationalist). Each faction scores only from its "native" colour but can *suppress* others. This is the cheapest high-value change in the whole review — it turns a bookkeeping track into an actual contest over what legitimacy *means*.

## 5. The hub strategy
**Produced?** Yes, through the Merchant Emirate's Safe Harbour and the "war within 2 spaces halves income" vulnerability. That's a real dramatisation of Beirut-then-Dubai-then-Doha. The risk is that Safe Harbour is under-tuned; at the table, hosting assets for a fee may simply be worse than playing your own game. Worth playtesting with an **escrow-scoring** twist: the hub scores VP proportional to the *total* value of assets hosted, so being useful to others is itself a win condition.

## 6. Fragmentation dynamics
**Produced?** Partially. The Contested Republic's A/B/C/D blocs and reconciliation track are thematically correct, but they model fragmentation only *inside* one faction. Historically Lebanon, Iraq, Syria, Yemen, Libya show fragmentation as a *regional* phenomenon — regions fracture, not just states.
**Fix:** Allow any region (not just the Contested Republic) to drop to a "Fragmented" status if Stability hits 0, at which point it stops yielding resources but becomes a proxy free-fire zone with outsized Influence rewards. This is closer to post-2011 reality.

## 7. Tech asymmetry and the ceiling of force
**Produced?** Nominally — the Settler Republic has a Tech ladder and a Deterrent action at Legitimacy cost. But nothing actively models the *ceiling*: nothing stops the Tech player from simply winning militarily and then buying Legitimacy back.
**Fix:** Introduce a **Pyrrhic Rule**: each Military action against a region permanently reduces the max regional Legitimacy the attacker can ever hold there. Overwhelming force should foreclose political settlement, not merely delay it. This is the single mechanical intervention most likely to dramatise the Lebanon 1982 / Iraq 2003 / Gaza lesson.

## 8. Normalisation whiplash
**Produced?** No. The rondel and trade-route mechanics reward slow, path-dependent relationships. The game has no affordance for an Abraham Accords moment — a realignment that was structurally impossible in Era N becoming standard in Era N+1.
**Fix:** Each Era, one random pair of factions gets a **Realignment Offer** card: if both accept, all prior hostility between them is wiped and a lucrative joint action unlocks, at the cost of one proxy relationship each. This is high-variance, which is correct; normalisations feel sudden because they are.

## Best and worst

**Best:** Proxy warfare. The combination of Stability, Information/attribution, and shared-fate scoring really does produce the right emergent behaviour — players will discover for themselves that destabilising a neighbour taxes their own trade.

**Worst:** Legitimacy competition. Everyone fights over one undifferentiated track, which is precisely the abstraction that launders the real politics. Not a fatal flaw, but the most urgent fix, and cheap.

## Era structure

5 eras at roughly a decade each is the right frame. **3 eras** would collapse the long arc — you need enough turns for a Petro player's institutions to rot, for sanctions to ratchet, for a proxy to metastasise. **7 eras** would over-simulate and break the 150-minute ceiling. The one adjustment: the eras should not be equal length. Model 1975–2025 as **1970s / 1980s–90s (doubled) / 2000s / 2010s / 2020s**, because the middle stretch is where most of the region's path dependencies were locked in.

## Episodes the mechanics literally cannot produce

- The death of Khomeini, Nasser, Arafat, or Rafsanjani — a leader dies and an entire faction's strategy pivots in one turn.
- The 1979 revolution — an ally becomes an enemy without any player's military action.
- Sudden collapse (USSR 1991, Qaddafi 2011) where a faction simply exits the board.
- October 2023-style rupture events that reset every diplomatic assumption.

**Recommendation:** add an **Upheaval** mechanic. Once per game per faction, a player may voluntarily trigger Upheaval (reset their special action, swap their private lever, pay a Legitimacy penalty). Additionally, one Era Card per game *forces* Upheaval on a random faction. Without this, CRESCENT models the region's slow pressures beautifully but cannot produce its ruptures — and the ruptures are the part players will most want to feel.
